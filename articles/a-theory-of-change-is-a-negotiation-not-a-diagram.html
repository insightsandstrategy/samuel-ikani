A Theory of Change Is a Negotiation, Not a Diagram

The most valuable theories of change are not drafted by consultants in hotel rooms. They are argued into existence by the people who will have to live with them.

A theory of change is the broader narrative that a logframe summarises. Where the logframe is a matrix, the theory of change is a story — a structured account of how and why a particular set of actions is expected to lead, through a chain of intermediate steps and under specified conditions, to a particular set of long-term outcomes.

It adds three things the logframe typically omits: an explicit account of the contextual factors that shape the pathway, a fuller treatment of the assumptions that link each step to the next, and an honest acknowledgement of the rival hypotheses that might also account for any change you observe.

A good theory of change contains a clearly stated long-term outcome, a backwards-mapped chain of intermediate outcomes, the interventions meant to make them more likely, the assumptions that must hold, a contextual analysis, rival explanations, and the signals that will tell you whether the theory is unfolding as expected.

How a Theory of Change Should Be Produced

What textbooks often under-emphasise is the process by which a theory of change should be developed.

A theory of change developed by a single consultant over two days is almost always inferior to one argued into existence collaboratively by the people who will implement the programme.

The collaborative process surfaces implicit assumptions, disagreements, contextual nuances, and uncomfortable questions that a solo author would miss. It also builds the shared ownership without which the theory of change will be ignored once the consultant leaves.

The Protocol I Keep Returning To

Convene ten to twenty people who together represent the implementing team, partners, intended beneficiaries, and a small number of outsiders who can ask difficult questions.

  • Define the long-term outcome in concrete terms.
  • Work backwards to identify the intermediate outcomes required to achieve it.
  • Interrogate the assumptions linking each step.
  • Identify rival explanations for any observed change.
  • Define the evidence the MERL system will need.
  • Treat the theory of change as a living document that evolves over time.

From the Field

During the development of a theory of change for a coalition-led anti-corruption project across six Nigerian states, one lesson emerged clearly: coalition alignment is often harder than technical design.

Civil society organisations, media actors, legal advocacy groups, and community accountability structures approached corruption from different perspectives. Through participatory workshops, stakeholder mapping, and problem-tree analysis, the coalition collectively identified weak citizen accountability, limited institutional transparency, and fear of retaliation as core drivers.

The process revealed that a theory of change is not merely a planning tool but a negotiation framework — one that builds shared ownership, clarifies assumptions, and aligns diverse actors toward measurable systemic change.

Logframes and Theories of Change Are Complementary

The logframe-versus-theory-of-change debate is a false choice. The theory of change is the deeper narrative; the logframe is the disciplined summary that supports planning and reporting.

The theory comes first. The logframe is derived from it.

If donor templates force you to begin with the logframe, reverse-engineer the theory of change as early as possible and use it as the working document for internal management.

The Learning Loop

Every theory of change rests on a judgement about plausibility. We cannot prove in advance that a chain of activities will produce a long-term outcome; we can only assess whether the chain is reasonable.

The role of MERL is not to manufacture false certainty but to test and refine plausibility over time, replacing assumption with evidence wherever possible.

A theory of change without a MERL system is just a story. A MERL system without a theory of change is just data. Together they form a learning loop.

Take It to Your Practice

Before your next theory-of-change workshop, write down who will be in the room. If the list does not include someone who can challenge the design and someone who will have to live with it, you are about to produce a diagram, not a negotiation.
